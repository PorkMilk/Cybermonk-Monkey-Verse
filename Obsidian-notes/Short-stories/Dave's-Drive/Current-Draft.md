
"That's gonna do it for me, folks. I'll be back soon. Next up is all classics all hour, and remember — I'll never leave you, because I'm always here."

"Wake up," he muttered, stretching as he reached over and changed the station. "Let's hear some news."

"—corn's trading at two-sixty a bushel, beans around five-eighty, winter wheat holding at three-forty on the board this morning. Suet steady. Blood meal up slightly. Human flesh down a touch on the week. Rounding out the morning report, hog futures unchanged, cotton holding firm."

David looked at the cloud-covered sky, trees blurring past the edge of his sight as the voice moved on to the weather.

"—expect clear skies through the morning hours, temperatures climbing to one-forty-seven by early afternoon across the region, dropping to near ninety by evening."

David stared at the radio so long the car drifted. He grabbed the wheel, breath snagging with it.

He shook his head and sat up straight.

"What the hell?" he said to no one.

The tone shifted, warmer now.

"—tickets are moving fast this year, organizers say, so don't wait if you want a seat for the skinning."

David's hand stopped on the volume knob.

"—and don't forget, the Ladies' Auxiliary potluck moves to Thursday this week, same time, same place, bring a covered dish. Founders Day parade lineup's posted down at the co-op."

He sighed, shoulders dropping an inch.

"Skinning," he said.

He turned the volume up instead of down.

The droning kept on, voice and music both blurring into the same flat hum. He caught himself somewhere past that, jaw slack, eyes open but pointed at nothing in particular.

"Music didn't work," Dave muttered. "News sucks too."

He reached toward the passenger seat without looking, patting around for a drink, a snack, anything. No drink in the cup holder. No snacks in the seat.

"Where's my—" He stopped himself, tried again. "When'd I stop last?"

He couldn't say. Dave didn't remember the last time he'd driven past a gas station, or the last time he'd seen another car.

He didn't know where he was going. That scared him less than the thought that came right after it. He didn't know where he'd come from.

He eased off the gas, hardly aware he'd done it.

He watched the mirror instead of the road for a second too long. Light was gathering back there, low and orange, threading up through the treeline like something catching.

He eased off further, and the light didn't wait for him. It came on faster than he was slowing, spreading along both sides of the road now, and he could see it was flame, real flame, climbing the treeline on both sides. A crackle rode in under the engine noise, then a low roar under that. Heat pressed through the glass, close enough to feel on his face.

He put his foot down. Hard.

The light bled back into the trees behind him, gone.
